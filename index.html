How Melbourne Got Its Coffee

Coffee grows nowhere in Australia. The country still imports A$659 million of raw beans a year, and Melbourne turned that dependence into one of the densest café cultures on earth.

The reputation is real. So are the numbers behind it.

Between 2002 and 2019 the City of Melbourne's café and restaurant count climbed from 886 to 1,771. The beans behind those venues arrive from 49 countries, and the CBD alone packs 383 of them into every square kilometre. This page follows the supply chain end to end: the equatorial farms that grow the beans, the $66 billion national industry they feed, the two-decade expansion that filled the city, the areas that absorbed it, and the record 2025 price shock now working its way down to the counter. (City of Melbourne, 2024; World Bank WITS, 2023)

01 The Global Journey

Where the beans come from

Every Melbourne coffee starts on the other side of the world. The plant needs a tropical climate Victoria does not have, so the whole ritual runs on imports. In 2023 Australia bought green beans from 49 countries and spent more than A$660 million on the raw crop, before a single bean was roasted.

The map traces where those beans grow, clustered in the narrow equatorial belt between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, and how they converge on one city. The origins are not interchangeable. Some send Australia bulk tonnage, others a smaller volume worth far more a kilogram. (World Bank WITS, 2023)

Growers vs suppliers

The fourth-biggest supplier is a small grower

Papua New Guinea is only the world's 17th-largest producer, farming roughly 930,000 bags a year, yet it is Australia's fourth-largest supplier by value. The slope chart shows its line climbing across far bigger growers as it moves from producer rank to supplier rank. Distance explains the jump: at about 3,500 km from Melbourne, PNG is the closest major origin, and the shorter shipping line undercuts Latin America on freight and transit time.

Vietnam is the mirror image. The world's second-largest producer at over 31 million bags a year, more than thirty times PNG's output, it ranks only fifth as a supplier here. Its robusta crop mostly feeds instant-coffee factories in Europe and Asia, not a market built on specialty arabica. (International Coffee Organization, 2018; World Bank WITS, 2023)

The takeaway

Brazil anchors the supply chain at more than a quarter of imports by value, but trade does not simply track production scale: Papua New Guinea ranks fourth as a supplier on proximity, not output. Those forty-nine origins feed a national industry whose place in the world looks nothing like its café-famous reputation. (World Bank WITS, 2023)

02 Australia in the World

A big reputation, a light habit

Those beans feed a far bigger story than a morning cup. Australia's cafés, restaurants and takeaway venues turn over more than $66 billion a year and employ close to 700,000 people, making hospitality one of the country's largest private employers. The country that once only bought green beans now earns a growing premium selling the craft back, exporting the specialty-roasting style its cafés helped define.

Yet Australians are not heavy coffee drinkers. At 4.6 kilograms a head a year the country sits near the bottom of the coffee-drinking nations, below Brazil, Italy and Germany, not just the Nordic leaders. The reputation rests on how the coffee is made, not how much is poured. (DFAT, 2025; International Coffee Organization, 2023; ABS, 2024)

From importer to exporter

Australia now roasts for the world

Earnings from roasted coffee and coffee products rose from A$48 million in 2006 to A$147 million in 2025, and the curve steepens at the end. It took fifteen years to clear A$86 million in 2021; the next four years added another A$60 million, with a single 24 per cent jump in 2023. Other countries are now paying a premium for the specialty-roasting style Melbourne helped define, turning a one-time pure importer into an exporter of the craft. (DFAT, 2025)

Reputation versus reality

A big reputation, a light habit

Finland drinks 12 kilograms a person a year and the Nordic countries fill the top of the table, but Brazil (5.8) and Italy (5.2) sit comfortably above Australia too. At 4.6 kilograms, Australia ranks third from last of the eighteen nations shown, ahead of only the United States and the United Kingdom.

It is a sharp corrective. Melbourne's coffee fame rests on how the coffee is made and served, not on how much of it the country drinks. (International Coffee Organization, 2023)

The takeaway

Australia drinks less per head than Brazil, Italy, even Germany, yet runs a $66 billion café industry and now exports its roasting craft for A$147 million a year. The edge was never volume; it is craft. And nowhere is that craft more concentrated than in Melbourne, where the next chapter begins. (ABS, 2024; DFAT, 2025; International Coffee Organization, 2023)

03 The Melbourne Boom

Twenty years, double the cafés

Once the beans land, the city takes over. The City of Melbourne has counted every café and restaurant each year since 2002, an unbroken twenty-two-year record. The count rose from 886 in 2002 to a peak of 1,771 in 2019, fell through the closures of 2020 to 2022, and has been rebuilding since.

The headline hides two quieter shifts. The growth years were not evenly spaced, and as the city added cafés the average café got smaller. The next two charts take each apart. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

Two curves, opposite directions

More cafés, but smaller ones

The bars count cafés each year, rising from 886 in 2002 to 1,771 in 2019 before the closures cut in. The red line, read off the right axis, runs the other way: average size fell from 96 seats in 2002 to 79 by 2024.

The scene did not scale up by building bigger rooms. It scaled out by building more of them, and smaller. Specialty coffee rewards a focused 30-seat room with one machine and a regular crowd over a 120-seat function space. Repeated across two decades, that preference dragged the average down even as the count climbed. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

Year by year

The boom came in bursts, not a steady climb

The 22-year average of about 4 per cent growth a year hides a wide spread. The fastest single year was 2005 at +8.6 per cent, with 2013 (+7.8%) and 2009 (+7.2%) close behind. By 2017 the pace had already slowed to under 1 per cent a year, well before the 2020 closures hit.

Three straight years of contraction, 2020 to 2022, erased almost a decade of gains. The rebound since is real but partial: at 1,645 cafés in 2024 the city is still short of its 2019 peak of 1,771. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

The takeaway

Melbourne's café scene grew almost without pause for two decades to a 2019 peak of 1,771. As it grew the typical café shrank from 96 seats to 79, smaller and more specialised. And the growth was lumpy: a few standout years, led by 2005, drove most of the net gain. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

04 The Neighbourhood

Where coffee took root

Cafés did not spread evenly. The census's closest measure of coffee venues is its "cafés and restaurants" category, so every count here is the best available proxy for the city's coffee scene. Each of central Melbourne's inner-city areas grew its own coffee character, shaped by street grid, residents and history. The CBD stands alone: at 383 venues per square kilometre it is nearly five times denser than the next area in line.

Density is only part of it. Some areas climbed the rankings over twenty years while others slipped, and the mix of many small venues against a few large ones swings sharply from one to the next. The three charts in this chapter read the same map three ways. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

The scene grew and reshuffled

Docklands climbed the ranks

Melbourne's count of these venues nearly doubled from 886 in 2002 to 1,645 in 2024, and the ranking reshuffled underneath that growth. Docklands shows it most sharply: 13 venues and eighth place in 2002, 157 and third by 2024, an elevenfold rise. Two decades of waterfront construction pulled tens of thousands of workers and residents in, and new venues followed them. The bump chart traces every area's rank year by year: most lines hold roughly flat, Docklands cuts upward across all of them, and the CBD never once loses first place. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

Many small, or a few large?

A few areas go big; most stay small

Each area takes a different shape. Southbank runs 125 venues, about a seventh of the CBD's count, yet they seat 129 each on average against the CBD's 76. The takeaway

Coffee clusters hardest in the CBD, denser than anywhere else, but the map keeps moving. Docklands grew from 13 venues to 157 in two decades, one of the city's sharpest neighbourhood shifts. Southbank stays few but oversized, and Carlton holds second place on count year after year on its student trade. (City of Melbourne, 2024)

05 The Price of Your Cup

What a cup now costs

Bean prices exploded. The cup did not keep up. The same Brazilian farms that opened this story were hit by drought through 2024 and 2025, pushing the world price of green coffee to the highest level ever recorded, robusta up about 169 per cent on its 2015 level at the peak. A flat white that ran about $3.50 a decade ago now rarely lands under $5.50, a real rise, but a far gentler one than the bean shock behind it.

Bean prices and the price Australians pay do not move together. Over the same decade Australian retail coffee climbed only about 72 per cent, slowly and smoothly, while beans lurched. The reason is on the receipt: green coffee is only a small slice of what a cup costs, so even a record bean price reaches the counter softened by roasting, wages, rent and milk. (FRED, 2025; ABS, 2025)

Thirty years of prices, one record high

2025 broke the record

Coffee has always been a volatile crop. Brazilian frosts and droughts sent prices soaring in 1997 and again in 2011. The spike that began in 2024 went further, pushing the global arabica price past every previous peak in the modern record on drought in Brazil and tight supply from Vietnam.

The long view is the point. This is not an ordinary rise off a low base; it is the most expensive coffee has ever been on the world market. (FRED, 2025)

The takeaway

The price of a cup is set far from the café, but it does not track the bean. Drought in Brazil, the origin that opened this story, pushed the global bean price to a record in 2025, yet Australian retail coffee rose only about 72 per cent over the decade against beans' 169 per cent peak. Green coffee is the smallest line on the receipt, so the café absorbs most of the shock before it ever reaches your cup. (FRED, 2025; ABS, 2025)

06 The Cost Behind the Counter

Where your $5.50 goes

Five fifty sounds like a lot for a coffee. Split across what actually goes into the cup, it is surprisingly little. The beans that crossed the world are one of the smallest lines at 56 cents. Labour and rent dominate: together they take $2.65, nearly half the cup and almost five times the cost of the beans.

On industry estimates the café keeps a net margin of about 7.6 per cent, roughly 42 cents a cup, well below the average Australian business. On these numbers the flat white is a thin-margin product. (ATO, 2024)

A broad-based squeeze

Every input got dearer

No single villain drove the $3.50 cup to $5.50. Between about 2019 and 2025 the beans nearly doubled (+75%), the steepest rise of any input, while milk rose a third and wages and rent climbed around a quarter each. In dollars the order flips: the extra labour added 30 cents to the cup, more than the extra beans' 24. Every input pushed up at once.

That is why cafés lifted prices and still kept little. Their own costs rose almost as fast as the menu board. (FRED, 2025; ABS, 2025)

From bean to cup to the price you pay, a city's story told in data.

Six chapters trace a single supply chain from origin to receipt. A handful of equatorial farms, led by Brazil, grow the beans that feed a $66 billion national industry, one that drinks little per head yet exports its craft to the world. A concentration of roasters in one of the most café-dense districts anywhere turns those beans into something with a distinct character, and a twenty-year market transformation filled every neighbourhood with smaller, more focused venues. And in the end, a global price shock works its way from the farm to the counter, landing in a $5.50 cup that the café barely profits from. Melbourne did not inherit its coffee culture. It built it, incrementally, over the better part of three decades.

About this visualisation

Why & Who

Designed for a general Australian audience that consumes coffee daily but has little visibility into the supply chain behind it. The aim is to turn scattered public records into a coherent story: from the farms that grow the beans to the streets that absorb the morning rush.

What & How

Public datasets from Australian and international government and trade sources, aggregated into lightweight CSV files and visualised with fifteen Vega-Lite charts: a world flow map and a city dot map over Melbourne's property-parcel fabric (the two required maps), alongside ranked bar charts, a slope chart, a diverging-bar growth chart, an area-and-line timeline, a dual-axis count-and-size chart, a bump chart, a bubble scatter, an indexed multi-line price chart, a long-run price area, a ranked lollipop, a coffee-mug donut and a grouped before-and-after bar. Each idiom was chosen to surface a specific insight the underlying data makes legible.
